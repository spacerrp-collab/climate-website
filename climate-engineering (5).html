Section 01 — Introduction

What is
Climate Engineering?

Climate engineering — also known as geoengineering — means using large-scale technology to change how Earth works, in order to slow down or reverse climate change.

Most climate plans focus on cutting greenhouse gas emissions or helping people cope with rising temperatures. Climate engineering is different — it proposes to directly change either how much heat Earth holds in, or what gases are in the air.

This idea has moved from the edges of science into serious government debate as global temperatures keep rising. Scientists, governments, and international groups are now asking whether these technologies could act as a backup plan alongside normal climate action.

Climate engineering falls into two main groups: Solar Radiation Management (SRM), which tries to reduce how much sunlight reaches Earth, and Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR), which pulls CO₂ directly out of the air or oceans.

"Geoengineering is not a substitute for reducing greenhouse gas emissions, but it may be a necessary complement to existing climate strategies."

— Royal Society, Geoengineering the Climate (2009)

The moral, political, and environmental risks are huge. Supporters say these tools could buy valuable time while the world moves away from fossil fuels. Critics warn that things could go wrong in unexpected ways, that governments might not agree on rules, and that it could take pressure off countries to actually reduce emissions — a risk known as "moral hazard".

Key Definitions

Climate Engineering: Using large-scale technology to change Earth's natural systems and reduce the effects of climate change.

Geoengineering: Another word for climate engineering; covers both SRM and CDR methods.

Solar Radiation Management (SRM): Ways to bounce sunlight away from Earth to cool it down — works fast but is only temporary.

Carbon Dioxide Removal (CDR): Ways to pull CO₂ out of the air — slower, but deals with the actual problem.

Moral Hazard: The worry that having a backup plan could make countries less likely to cut emissions, making things worse in the long run.

Section 02 — Classification

Types of
Climate Engineering

The field splits into two very different ideas: one that blocks sunlight from reaching Earth, and one that removes the gases that are causing it to warm up.

SRM — Solar Radiation Management

Reflecting Sunlight Away

SRM methods try to reduce how much sunlight Earth soaks up, which cools the planet without removing CO₂ from the air. They work fast — within months — but need to be kept running forever, or temperatures would jump back up quickly.

  • Stratospheric Aerosol Injection (SAI): Spraying tiny reflective particles (like sulfur dioxide) high in the atmosphere to copy the cooling effect that happens after a volcanic eruption.
  • Marine Cloud Brightening (MCB): Spraying sea-salt particles to make low clouds more reflective, so they bounce more sunlight back into space.
  • Space Mirrors: Placing giant sun-blocking panels in Earth's orbit to reduce incoming sunlight — still mostly a theory.
  • Surface Albedo Enhancement: Painting roofs, roads, or crops white so they reflect more sunlight instead of absorbing heat.
CDR — Carbon Dioxide Removal

Removing CO₂ from the Air

CDR methods tackle the actual cause of climate change by pulling CO₂ out of the air and storing it safely. They are slower to show results but offer a longer-lasting fix — dealing with the problem itself rather than just the symptoms.

  • Direct Air Capture (DAC): Machines (including artificial trees) that filter CO₂ straight from the air using chemical reactions.
  • Bioenergy with CCS (BECCS): Growing plants that absorb CO₂, burning them for energy, and trapping the carbon that comes out.
  • Ocean Fertilisation: Adding iron or nutrients to oceans to help tiny sea plants grow, which absorb CO₂ as they live and sink.
  • Enhanced Weathering: Spreading crushed rock minerals on land or in the ocean to speed up the natural process of absorbing CO₂.

Section 03 — Case Study

Artificial Trees &
Carbon Capture in the USA

The United States is leading the world in Direct Air Capture technology — building large machines known as "artificial trees" that pull CO₂ straight out of the air.

Core Case Study · United States · CDR Technology

Direct Air Capture: The Heirloom & Stratos Projects

From Climeworks' Stratos plant in Texas to Heirloom Carbon's limestone-based system in Louisiana, the US is building the world's most ambitious carbon removal machines — with billions of dollars of government funding behind them.

How Artificial Trees Work

01

Air Intake & Capture

Large fans pull air through a special chemical filter — usually potassium hydroxide or solid amine material — that sticks to CO₂ and holds it. Real trees do this with their leaves; artificial trees use engineered chemicals instead.

02

Heating & Release

The filter, now full of CO₂, is heated to between 80–900°C (depending on the machine type). This releases a clean stream of pure CO₂ gas. The filter is then cleaned and reused.

03

Storage or Use

The captured CO₂ is either locked underground permanently in rock (a process called geological storage), turned into synthetic fuel, or sold for commercial uses like carbonated drinks.

Key Results & Progress

Climeworks opened Stratos — the world's biggest working DAC plant — in Ector County, Texas in 2024. It can remove up to 36,000 tonnes of CO₂ per year. Heirloom Carbon launched its first commercial site using a limestone-based process, while the US Department of Energy is funding four large regional DAC hubs across the country, with a goal of removing millions of tonnes of CO₂ each year by 2035.

The Inflation Reduction Act (2022) gave companies a $180 tax credit for every tonne of CO₂ captured through direct air capture — later increased — making the US the most supportive government in the world for this technology. This led to a wave of investment from companies like Microsoft, Stripe, and Shopify, who signed deals to buy carbon removal credits.

⚠ Concerns & Criticisms

⚡

Huge Energy Use

DAC plants need a lot of electricity to heat the filters. If that power comes from fossil fuels, the carbon released by running the machine could cancel out the CO₂ it removes.

💸

Very Expensive

At $300–600 per tonne of CO₂, DAC is far more costly than simply avoiding emissions in the first place. To reach the scale needed, costs would have to drop by around ten times.

📏

Not Big Enough Yet

To make a real dent in atmospheric CO₂, DAC plants would need to be thousands of times larger than everything currently built and running.

🔄

False Sense of Security

Some critics worry that carbon capture gives governments and companies an excuse not to cut emissions — when reducing emissions at source remains the most reliable long-term fix.

Section 05 — Personal Evaluation

Is Climate Engineering
the Answer?

Now that you have looked at the science, the case studies, and examples from around the world — what do you think? Use this section to write your own thoughts and conclusions.

Climate engineering puts humanity in a difficult position. The technologies that could protect us from the worst effects of climate change also carry serious risks of their own. The artificial trees in Texas capture CO₂ with impressive accuracy — but each tonne removed costs hundreds of dollars and requires huge amounts of electricity, showing just how far the gap is between what is scientifically possible and what can realistically be done at the scale the crisis requires.

Looking at the world picture, there is a wide range of approaches being tried, but none of them is enough on its own. SRM methods can cool the planet quickly, but they are only temporary, they affect some regions more than others, and they carry the serious risk of a sudden temperature spike — known as "termination shock" — if they were ever stopped suddenly. CDR methods last longer, but they work more slowly, cost more, and need far more land and energy.

The key question here is not just about science — it is about politics and fairness: who gets to decide when to use these tools? Who suffers if something goes wrong? And does working on these technologies make it easier — or harder — for the world to do what is really needed, which is to stop burning fossil fuels?
